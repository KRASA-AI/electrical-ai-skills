# Commercial Lighting Incentives & Energy-Code Drivers — 2026

**Scope:** Practical reference for an electrical contractor pricing or pitching a commercial LED retrofit, tenant-improvement (TI) lighting package, or new-construction lighting controls scope in 2026. Pulls together the federal tax driver (Section 179D), the dominant state energy-code driver (California Title 24 2025 / ASHRAE 90.1-2026 lighting controls), and the utility-rebate landscape.

**Last updated in this repo:** 2026-04-25 (landscape monitor — 179D construction-start deadline, Title 24 2025 effective dates, 2026 utility-rebate trends).

This document is a working summary, not legal or tax advice. Verify all program rules with the specific utility, AHJ, and a tax professional before quoting a number to a customer.

---

## 1. Section 179D — Federal Energy-Efficient Commercial Buildings Deduction

### 1.1 What it is

Section 179D of the Internal Revenue Code allows the owner (or, on tax-exempt-owned buildings, the **designer of record** — which can include the electrical contractor when the contractor wrote the lighting controls spec) to claim a deduction for energy-efficient lighting, HVAC, or building-envelope improvements in commercial buildings.

### 1.2 The hard deadline that matters in 2026

**Construction must begin on or before June 30, 2026** for the project to qualify under the current 179D rules. The deadline is on **construction start**, not on placed-in-service. To "begin construction" the IRS considers either:

- **Significant physical work test** — actual on-site or off-site fabrication / installation work has begun, or
- **5% safe harbor** — at least 5% of the total project cost has been paid or incurred, AND the project demonstrates "continuous progress" thereafter.

After June 30, 2026 the program sunsets under the rules in force as of this writing. Do not assume an extension.

### 1.3 Deduction amounts (current published rates)

- **Up to $5.81 per square foot** with prevailing-wage and apprenticeship requirements met (2025 published rate).
- Lower per-square-foot rates apply when prevailing-wage / apprenticeship requirements are not met.
- The deduction scales with energy savings versus a reference baseline (ASHRAE 90.1) — partial deductions are available for projects that don't hit the full performance target.

### 1.4 What this means for the contractor

- Any commercial-lighting pitch issued in **April–June 2026** has a real urgency story attached to it: the customer's tax position changes if the project doesn't break ground by June 30. Lead with the deadline, not with adjectives.
- **Designer of record** allocations on tax-exempt-owned buildings (schools, municipal, federal, non-profit) are an under-claimed revenue line for electrical contractors who actually wrote the controls spec. Talk to the tax-exempt owner's tax preparer before the project closes out.
- LED-only retrofits with the shortest lead times (~4–6 weeks total) are the most plausible "still beat the deadline" scope. Major HVAC or envelope work generally won't make the cutoff if not already in motion.

### 1.5 Anti-overpromise rules

- Do not quote a 179D dollar figure without the project's actual conditioned square footage, the modeled energy savings, and the prevailing-wage / apprenticeship status confirmed.
- Do not promise designer-of-record allocation without the building owner's allocation letter — the deduction belongs to the building owner by default.
- Do not assume the project clears 25% lighting power density (LPD) reduction (one of the partial-deduction thresholds) without an energy model.

## 2. California Title 24 (2025 update) — Effective for Permits Filed January 1, 2026

### 2.1 What it is

California's Title 24, Part 6 Building Energy Efficiency Standards. The 2025 update is in force for any permit application submitted on or after **January 1, 2026**. The change set is largely about lighting controls, not just bulb wattage — the controls scope on a TI is now the largest single line item on most retrofit packages.

### 2.2 Lighting Power Density (LPD) reductions

- **Office spaces:** 0.65 W/sq ft (down from 0.75 W/sq ft)
- **Retail spaces:** 0.9 W/sq ft (down from 1.0 W/sq ft)
- Other space types reduced proportionally — verify against the published Chapter 6 of the 2025 Standards.

### 2.3 Required lighting controls (commercial)

- **Manual controls** at the room or zone level
- **Multilevel lighting** (continuous dimming or stepped levels)
- **Automatic shutoff** (occupant sensor or time-switch); control zones generally ≤ 5,000 sq ft (≤ 20,000 sq ft permissible in malls and warehouse-type industrial space)
- **Automatic daylight-responsive controls** in any zone meeting the daylit-area definition (general lighting in those zones must dim as daylight increases)
- **Demand-responsive controls** for buildings of qualifying size

### 2.4 Acceptance Test Technician (ATT) certification

- **Certificate of Installation** plus **Certificate of Acceptance** are required, both signed by a certified Acceptance Test Technician (ATT). Most jurisdictions will not sign off the lighting scope without these documents on file.
- Many electrical contractors carry an in-house ATT (or contract with one) — this is a billable line item on TI retrofit pitches.

### 2.5 ASHRAE 90.1-2026 alignment

ASHRAE 90.1-2026 lighting-controls requirements largely mirror Title 24 2025 (the IECC lineage). Many non-California states have lighting-control requirements driven by their adoption of ASHRAE 90.1; the contractor's pitch should call out which standard the AHJ enforces.

### 2.6 Inspection-failure consequences

Failure to install required controls can result in:
- Failed inspection, permit delay, denied certificate of occupancy
- Forced post-install rework (controls + commissioning) at the contractor's expense if specified incorrectly
- Customer-facing schedule slip

A retrofit pitch should assume controls are mandatory and price them in. The "we can save you the controls cost" sales path is not legal in any 2025-Title-24 or 90.1-2026 jurisdiction on most space types.

## 3. Utility-Rebate Landscape — 2026 Snapshot

### 3.1 Coverage

- Roughly **75% of the United States** has access to an active commercial-lighting rebate program in 2026 (down ~2 percentage points year-over-year).
- Rebate values typically offset **15–40% of total project cost**, sometimes higher when advanced controls are bundled.

### 3.2 Structural shifts in 2026

- **6% shift away from flat per-unit rebates** (per lamp / per fixture) toward **performance-based** rebates (watts saved, kWh saved). Implication: the contractor's pitch must include the actual energy-savings model, not just the fixture count.
- **Retroactive (post-install) rebates have nearly disappeared.** If the contractor installs first and applies later, the money is generally gone. **Pre-approval is mandatory** in nearly every active program in 2026.
- **DLC-qualified products** (DesignLights Consortium Qualified Product List) and **ENERGY STAR–certified products** are required by most programs. Substitutions made in the field — even to functionally identical fixtures — can void the rebate.

### 3.3 Tenant-Improvement (TI) lighting incentive programs (example: Puget Sound Energy)

- PSE's BLti (Business Lighting Tenant Improvement) program runs **April 1, 2026 – April 30, 2027** with project completion required by **November 1, 2027**. Other utilities have analogous TI-specific tracks; verify the dates and forms on each utility's commercial portal.
- TI projects typically involve a major lighting remodel triggered by a new tenant or change of use. The contractor pulls the rebate paperwork on the customer's behalf as part of the package.

### 3.4 Bundling rules

- Many programs offer **bonus incentives** when controls (occupancy sensing, daylight-responsive dimming, networked systems) are bundled into the LED scope. Designing the pitch around control bundles often clears 25–35% of project cost when the bonus applies.
- "Flip the switch" simple LED swaps without controls are increasingly rebate-disqualified outside of small-business direct-install programs.

### 3.5 Pitch-side rules

- Always cite the rebate program by name and current published cap, not by an estimated dollar figure.
- Always confirm pre-approval status before quoting a final price (the rebate value is a deduction from the customer's net cost, not a discount the contractor controls).
- Always include the form-filing time as a deliverable in the proposal — it's a real billable activity.

## 4. Cross-Reference for Skill Outputs

When an AI skill produces a commercial-lighting retrofit pitch, the skill should:

1. Pull the 179D deadline (§ 1.2 above) into the urgency framing **only when the contractor's intake says the project will start before June 30, 2026**. After the deadline passes, drop this section entirely from skill output.
2. Pull the Title 24 / ASHRAE 90.1 controls list (§ 2.3 above) into the scope detail **only when the AHJ enforces one of those codes**. In jurisdictions still on older energy codes, downscope to the actual mandatory list.
3. Pull the utility-rebate framing (§ 3 above) into the cost section **only when the contractor's intake names a specific utility and program**. Never invent a program name or cite a placeholder rebate amount.
4. Cross-reference `nec-2026-key-changes.md` for the underlying NEC compliance pieces (e.g., emergency outdoor disconnect, qualified-persons mandate) when the lighting scope touches service equipment or new branch circuits.
